Data Science 1 Project: Instagram Analysis

As the amount of people and popularity on and of social media rises, we can see that people's ten minutes of fame are becoming more than a once in a lifetime occurrence. Leading to the wandering of whether there’s a way to artificially become more influential or popular online. Or in other words, is there a positive, statistically significant correlation between the number of followers, average likes, total number of likes, or number of posts an influencer has and their influence score? I approached answering this question through using a data table found on Kaggle (Top Influencers Crushing On Instagram), which contained a lot of data about Instagramers, including their influence score, number of followers, average likes, total number of likes, and number of posts (which are the five stats used for this analysis). After some data wrangling and analysis, I graphed the relationships between influence score and number of followers, average likes, total number of likes, and number of posts. With this showing that the number of followers have a positive correlation to influence score, whilst the average likes, total likes, and number of posts have a negative correlation to influence score. We can interpret these trends to mean that the best way to increase influence score is through increasing followers, whilst avoiding posting or getting likes. However, none of these trends or conclusions are valid, as the table under the graphs show that all Pearson Correlation Coefficients between data aren’t higher than the threshold of 0.5 (with the highest being the 0.365428 correlation between followers and influencer score), and therefore the data isn’t correlated enough for a statistically legitimate trend to be seen. On further analysis of the other data in Kaggle's data sheet, I found that rank of influencer could be a variable the influences the influence score (with the higher the rank leading to a higher score). At the end of the day, number of followers and rank are most likely to positively correlate with influence score, but I need more conclusive data.
